Why modern tea brands exploded: HEYTEA, Chagee, Chabaidao, and the Starbucks-like urban ritual

2026-03-16

Modern tea brands did not explode simply because young consumers wanted sweet drinks. What really happened was a re-engineering of tea for urban daily life. Tea became photogenic, queue-worthy, giftable, highly brandable, and easy to insert into office breaks, mall walks, dates, and social-media posting. Brands such as HEYTEA, Chagee, and Chabaidao succeeded because they turned beverages into a repeatable emotional and social routine.

This is where the comparison with Starbucks becomes useful. Starbucks did not only sell coffee. It sold a recognizable urban pause, a branded everyday ritual, and a stable way to make beverage consumption signify lifestyle. China’s modern tea brands did something similar for tea—while remaining more local in ingredients, fruit use, milk textures, tea bases, visual language, and speed of product iteration.

Why was the new wave stronger than older milk-tea chains?

Earlier milk-tea chains had already built consumer habits, but many were still positioned as cheap convenience drinks. The new generation reworked the entire experience: ingredients, store design, packaging, naming, display, queue culture, and social-media circulation. A drink was no longer just something sweet and cold. It became a visible consumer choice, a photographed object, and a small urban event.

In this sense, even waiting in line became part of the product. Scarcity, visibility, and participation fed the category’s rise.

What do HEYTEA, Chagee, and Chabaidao each represent?

HEYTEA became an emblem of the new tea era by combining upgraded ingredients, dramatic queue culture, premiumized presentation, and strong store identity. Chagee leaned more explicitly into Chinese cultural imagination and branded tea identity, giving the category a stronger “Eastern” visual narrative. Chabaidao represents a more scalable, everyday, broad-market pathway: highly repeatable, visible, and integrated into ordinary city consumption.

They are different in tone, but structurally similar in what they achieved: they returned tea to the center of urban youth life.

How did they change young people’s lives?

These brands changed dating routines, office habits, group ordering, mall behavior, social posting, and low-stakes self-reward. A cup of tea drink became a way to meet, apologize, celebrate, survive the afternoon, or leave with something in hand after wandering through a retail district. More deeply, they changed tea language itself. Even highly commercial drinks familiarized huge numbers of young consumers with jasmine, oolong, pu-erh, milk cap, tea base, roast, and other ideas once confined to more traditional tea settings.

That is why this category matters so much. It is not simply a sugary detour away from tea culture. It is one of the strongest contemporary routes through which tea re-entered the vocabulary of youth.

Why this topic matters in a tea culture site

A tea site that talks only about classical history and never about the drinks dominating malls, delivery platforms, and young people’s social life will feel incomplete. Modern tea brands are not the opposite of tradition. They are tradition reassembled inside branding, logistics, visual culture, and the platform economy. To explain them is to explain how tea continues to change in real time.

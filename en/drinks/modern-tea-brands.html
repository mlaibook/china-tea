Why fresh tea brands exploded: HEYTEA, Chagee, Chabaidao, and the Starbucks parallel

2026-03-16

Modern tea drink brands did not become huge only because young people suddenly liked sweet drinks. They rose because they combined tea, retail design, urban aspiration, social media visibility, and fast-moving consumer branding into one highly scalable format. Brands such as HEYTEA, Chagee, and Chabaidao became more than beverage sellers. They became lifestyle signals, meeting points, and everyday luxury products priced just high enough to feel upgraded but still broadly reachable.

There is a structural comparison with Starbucks: both models sell not only a drink, but a repeatable urban ritual. Both turn beverage consumption into identity, environment, and routine. But Chinese fresh tea brands are also distinctly local. They are more visually tied to Chinese tea ingredients, seasonal fruit, milk textures, and youth-oriented menu experimentation.

How did the category explode?

The category grew by improving ingredients, store design, packaging, and queue-driven visibility. Earlier milk tea chains had already built consumer habits, but the new wave reframed the whole experience as cleaner, fresher, more photogenic, and more brandable. Social media accelerated the effect: a drink was now an image, a location tag, and a small public statement of taste.

What is the Starbucks parallel?

Like Starbucks, these brands turned a beverage into a third-space-adjacent routine. Even where stores are smaller or more takeaway-oriented, they still offer a branded urban pause. They also trained consumers to accept premium pricing for an experience wrapped in design, consistency, and social meaning. The difference is that fresh tea brands often localize more aggressively around tea bases, fruit, cream toppings, Chinese cultural references, and youth aesthetics.

How did they affect young people?

They changed habits of meeting, posting, gifting, queuing, and casual spending. For many young consumers, ordering tea drinks became part of dating, office breaks, friendship maintenance, and mall life. The category also reintroduced tea vocabulary into youth culture. Even highly commercial drinks still familiarized millions of people with terms such as jasmine tea, oolong, pu-erh, or milk cap.
